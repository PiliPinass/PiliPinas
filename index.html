WhyVotingMatters


What is the role of voting in a democracy?

Voting is not just a right—it’s a responsibility. Voting gives a free, just, and fair society. It gives people the power to choose leaders who will represent their interests, and create policies that affect everyone.

Why is voting important?

Ensures your voice is heard, and the people's needs are attended to.
Holds leaders accountable for their actions.
Helps shape a better future for the country.


Characteristics of a Responsible Voter

We hold to future in the palm of our hands, we must be a responsible voter. And a responsible voter does:

Research about the candidates – We must understand their platforms, achievements, and credibility. To know if they can carry the pressure and power of the position they are running for.
Verifies information – We must fact-check news and avoid propaganda. The media is known for biased information, so we must be cautious before trusting any information.
Does not sell their vote – We must value our right to vote and refuse bribery.
Encourages others to vote wisely – We must educate family and friends on making informed decisions and share reliable news sources.

Steps to Making an Informed Decision Before Voting


Here are 4 steps to ensure you will vote wisely:


Step 1: Research the Candidates

What are their policies and plans? Have they fulfilled past promises? Do they have a clean track record? Why? Many candidates make big promises for big changes, but not all deliver. Researching their backgrounds, achievements, and platforms ensures that you vote for someone truly qualified, and trustworthy.

Informed voting prevents corrupt or incompetent leaders from winning.

How to Avoid Election Misinformation and Propaganda


Biased and fake news are everywhere, with a tap of our fingers we hold every possible information. That is why we must be wary of this information. To avoid them we must follow these give:

Fact-Check Before Sharing

Verify the source (Is it from a trusted news site?)
Cross-check with multiple sources
Look for fact-checking labels (e.g., Vera Files, Rappler Fact Check)
Be cautious of sensational or misleading headlines


Beware of Common Propaganda Tactics

Overpromising candidates ("I will eliminate corruption in one year!")
Fake surveys and manipulated statistics
Deepfake videos and edited photos

Voter Assistance Information


Ensuring a smooth and hassle-free voting experience starts with preparation. Here’s everything you need to know to avoid common problems on Election Day.


How to Register to Vote


(If registration is still open, follow these steps to make sure you are eligible to vote!)


1. Check if you’re eligible


Verify the source (Is it from a trusted news site?)
Cross-check with multiple sources
A resident of your voting district for at least six months before the election

2. Find your local COMELEC office


Verify the source (Is it from a trusted news site?)
Cross-check with multiple sources

3. Prepare your documents


Valid ID (Government-issued ID such as passport, driver’s license, postal ID, or student ID)
Proof of residence (if required)

4. Go to your local registration center


Registration is done in person at your designated COMELEC office.
Expect biometric capture (fingerprints, photo, and signature).

5. Get your voter’s registration stub


This serves as proof that you are a registered voter.


Important: Check COMELEC’s website for registration deadlines. Register early to avoid last-minute crowds!

How to Find Your Precinct on Election Day


1. Check your voter status

Visit the COMELEC website or local election office to verify your polling station.

2. Locate your assigned precinct

Some precincts may change, so confirm before election day.
COMELEC often provides online precinct finders—use them!

3. Arrive early

Polls are usually open from 6:00 AM to 7:00 PM (confirm with COMELEC for exact hours).
Avoid peak hours (midday) to skip long lines.

4. Know your rights

If your name is missing from the voter list but you are a registered voter, ask the election officers for assistance.
Persons with disabilities (PWDs), senior citizens, and pregnant women are usually given priority lanes.

Common Voting Day Problems and Solutions



Problem: You can’t find your name on the voter list.
Solution:

Ask an election officer for help; they can check the master list.
If you transferred residence, ensure you are in the correct precinct.

Problem: You lost your voter’s registration stub.
Solution:

Your name should still be on the voter list.
Bring a valid ID to verify your identity.

Problem: Someone is trying to influence or intimidate voters.
Solution:

Report immediately to election officers or watchdog groups like NAMFREL (National Citizens’ Movement for Free Elections).

Problem: The voting machine malfunctions.
Solution:

Report it to the election officer immediately.
Wait for instructions, but do not leave without voting.
Problem: You accidentally marked your ballot incorrectly.
Solution:

In some cases, you may request a replacement ballot (rules vary per election).
Double-check before shading to avoid mistakes.
